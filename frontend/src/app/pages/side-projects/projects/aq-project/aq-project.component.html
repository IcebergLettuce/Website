I have the impression that SaaS has been on everyone's lips in the tech industry over the last few years. Every founder, entrepreneur, or engineer I spoke to was keen to offer software as a service. Lately, that seems to have changed. Nowadays, everyone wants to build a platform.... you talk about platforms for ml, medical informatics, and commerce - just to name a few. When asked about the specific problem the platform solves or how the platform adds value, the answers are often very vague.

It becomes even more unclear when you ask about the definition of a platform. How is it different from SaaS?

However, I am not in a position to give an answer to this question. In my view, a platform is something like a workbench that should enable and empower people to create value. The platform owner should receive a fair share of this value creation for providing and maintaining the workbench. It is important to emphasize that value creation does not necessarily mean monetary value. Let's say you use a platform to improve the workflow of a software development team.In that case, I also consider that as value creation.
With this in mind, we can now dive into the ML space and discuss some approaches.

If you follow ML/AI research, you probably have noticed that the algorithms are applied to numerous problem domains. Just have a look at Computer Vision (CV) which is successfully used to:

  • CT and MRI analysis
  • Cancer detection
  • Pedestrian detection
  • ...
Each use case is a massive business case with lots of potential for success. Just imaging being the company that can provide an accurate cancer detection service. Theoretically, you can serve anyone who has an MRI/CT image... a vast market.

Now we have our business case, and the question is, what is next? How can we align our engineering efforts with our business goals? Should we make a SaaS solution or go for a more "platform" approach?

This is where the ML Quadrant comes into play. The ML Quadrant helps you categorize your ML Service into four categories among two dimensions.

One dimension is the domain knowledge required to build a performant ML model. I claim that there are problems that don't need a lot of domain knowledge and that one can be successful by just applying models out of the box. This is also the case if there is not a lot of domain knowledge available. The only approach is to use a lot of computing power and try many different models with different parameter configurations. I would say that many trading algorithms are of that kind. None really understands short-term market dynamics... thus, one has to experiment a lot. On the other hand, assume creating an ML model for a medical use case. Without profound medical knowledge, you probably have difficulty building a model outperforming your competitors. Also, the requirements for such a model are entirely different. For a medical ML model, explainability and trustworthiness will be exceptionally important. This might be neglectable for a trading model as long it is profitable.

The second dimension is integrability. There, one can distinguish between individual and general. What does that mean? Assume you built an ML model to stratify risks for health insurance. To provide the model to your customer, you need to integrate it deeply into their infrastructure. Every insurance has a different infrastructure, so you always need to adapt. Additionally, the insurances need to adapt their business process to benefit from your ML model. This is always associated with risk, and it will be a challenge to convince their leadership to buy-in. On the contrary, let us assume we offer an ML model to predict the performance of your social media activities. This is way easier to integrate. The whole social media business is fully digitized. Your customer subscribes to the service, passes the URL o the social media account, and your algorithm can start fetching the data.

For each category, there is different leverage to compete in the market.
For each category, there is a different way to engineer your system.
For each category, there is a different mindset you need to apply.
For each category, there is a different...

The ML Quadrant supports you in structuring this design process and finding a focus for your ML platform.

Important: I do not claim that these strategies are mutually exclusive, and I think a mature ML platform can cover all of them. Nevertheless, we have to start somewhere. And there is definitely room to improve the ML Quadrant as a tool. This should just sketch a possible approach to structure the design process.
